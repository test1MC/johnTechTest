Introduction to Bank Data Modules

 

Bank Data Analytics provides a robust view of bank performance across each of the five modules to enhance balance performance tracking and planning:

Bank Data Analytics Module Module Description
Total Balances Dynamic view of total in-scope balances with ability to segment by time, geography, product group, name or any other segmentation variables Will also provide insights to period end accounts, rates, and other metrics.

Balance Flows

 Dynamic view of balance flows (changes) which led to fluctuations in total balances over time. Displays Acquisition, Switch-In, Change to Existing, Switch Out and Attrition flows, as well as their more granular drill-down components.
Balance Waterfall Identifies the balance flows that contributed to balance or account change between two periods.
Switch Balances Dynamic view of customer level switch analysis identifies flow of balances both to different products within the same product group, as well as across product groups or other portfolio segmentation dimensions.
Maturing Balances Provides past and future maturity schedule for term products, as well as important rate and balance metrics regarding maturing term products (APY, reference rates, relative rate of last renewal, APY of balances maturing, etc.).
Historic Roll Rates Shows past term product "roll" behavior at the time of maturity, breaking out roll, switch, stay and attrition percentages and values. Also identifies rate relationships between balances that rolled or switched terms.

Understanding Balance Flow Definitions

 

Balance Waterfall

 

Bank Data Analytics allows you to analyze balance flows across portfolio dimensions

The balance In-flows generally require a new account to be opened or a qualifying product change to an existing account.
Balance Flow Description
Acquisition Balances in new accounts funded with entirely new-to-bank money (e.g., no corresponding diminishment of the same customer’s other existing accounts).
Switch In (Switch) The portion of balances in new accounts that were funded directly from one or more of the customer’s other accounts.
Switch In (Augmentation)

New-to-bank balances in new accounts that were at least partially funded directly from one or more of the customer’s other accounts1 .
1Switch In can also occur on an account whose product group, product name, or term changes from month to month.
Balance level change for existing accounts is captured in the Change to Existing balance flow.
Balance Flow Description
Change to Existing Net augmentation/diminishment for existing accounts not participating in one of the other balance flows1.
1Existing accounts are generally those accounts that exist with positive balances from one month to the next, and do not change customer ID, account ID, product or term.
The out-flow designation depends on whether an account is funding another new account for the same customer.
Balance Flow Description
Switch Out (Switch) Balances in accounts that directly funded a new account held by the same customer in a single month-over-month period.
Switch Out (Diminishment) Balances that left a Switch Out account, but were not cannibalized by the corresponding Switch In account(s), and presumably left the bank.
Attrition Balances in accounts that closed without funding any new accounts associated with the same customer.
Balance Flow vs. Granular Balance Flows

 

The standard balance flows can be decomposed into granular balance flows to provide a more nuanced view into customer behavior.
Balance Flow Granular Balance Flow Notes

Acquisition

(Origination)

 Customer Acquisition Acquisition balances resulting from a new customer to the bank. These three granular flows compose “new-to-bank” deposits.
Account Acquisition Acquisition balances resulting from an existing customer opening a new account.

Switch In

(Origination)

Switch In (Augmentation) Switch In balances that have been augmented and are new to the bank.

Switch In

(Origination)

Switch In (Switch) Switch In balances that are switched directly from another account. The Switch (Switch) flows are pure cannibalization and represent deposits that have moved directly between accounts.
Switch Out Switch Out (Switch) Switch Out balances that switched directly into a new account. The Switch (Switch) flows are pure cannibalization and represent deposits that have moved directly between accounts.
Switch Out Switch Out (Diminishment) Switch Out balances that did not appear in the new account, and presumably left the bank.   These three granular flows compose deposits that are leaving the bank.
  Attrition Customer Attrition Attrition balances resulting from a customer leaving the bank.
Account Attrition Attrition balances resulting from a customer closing an account but retaining other deposit accounts.
  Change to Existing Augmentation Total balance increase for existing accounts that augmented their balances. Net augmentation / diminishment for existing accounts.
Diminishment Total balance decrease for existing accounts that diminished their balances.
       

 

Understanding Balance Flow Calculations

 

 
Account Level Examples

 

Customer Acquisition and Attrition are the most straightforward flows.
If there is only movement within existing accounts, the flow is considered Change to Existing.
For customers with multiple accounts, we can tag the flow of funds from existing accounts to new accounts as Switch.
  • PriceTek methodology allows banks to track the flow of funds between multiple accounts across months.
  • In this particular example, to determine which of the customer’s new accounts was funded with the $10,000 decrease in existing accounts, we apply a hierarchy that says that the 0-9 Month CD account is more likely than the Savings account to have been funded by the 10-15 Month CD account
If balances fluctuate across multiple products, we apply a hierarchy (click here to view your institutional hierarchy) to determine the likely source product/destination product for the funds.
  • To determine what portion of the customer’s total $100,000 decrease in existing accounts ended up in the new Promotional Money Market account, we apply a hierarchy that says that the Interest Checking is the most likely to switch, followed by Savings, and finally the 10-15-Month CD.
  • In PriceTek, the $50,000 switched into the Promotional Money Market can be broken out into $20,000 switched from Interest Checking and $30,000 switched from Savings (at portfolio level).
  • Similarly, the $40,000 switched out of Savings can be cut as $30,000 switched to the Promotional Money Market and $10,000 diminished (at the portfolio level).

Handling Negative Balances

The way we handle accounts with negative balances varies by product:
  • “Transactional” products (e.g., checking) are considered “open” if they exist in the data at all, regardless of balance.
    • Within this category, we temporarily break negative balance accounts into two sub-accounts: an account with zero balance, and an account with negative balance.
    • This allows for us to use that transactional account in balance flows if, for example, it moves from positive to negative balance (or vice-versa), by capturing the positive-to-zero change as part of the flow.
    • We then re-insert the “negative” portion once flows have been calculated and force it into the “Change to Existing” flow group so that overall balance levels match across modules.
    • Zero-balance accounts are also included in counts of all accounts so that these counts will match across modules.
  • “Non-transactional” products (e.g., CDs) are only considered “open” if they have a positive balance in the data.
    • These accounts are considered “new” if they move from a negative or zero balance to a positive balance.
    • In this case, we remove both negative and zero balance accounts from balance flows entirely, so that the account doesn’t “exist” for the purposes of flows in months where it is negative.
Note: Any accounts that have a close date present in the data will be excluded across modules, regardless of the above conditions.
 
 
Captured as Switch Rules

We flag existing accounts as “new” and capture them as Switch when they experience a qualifying product change.

This methodology is designed to capture customer initiated changes to their deposit portfolio in order to accurately calculate cannibalization. The definition of a qualifying change in product information is highly customizable. Click here to view the Captured as Switch rules for your institution.

Additionally, there are some exceptions to the product change rules designed primarily to capture movement in and out of promotions/specials. Click here to view the Product Change Rule Exceptions created for your institution.

The Switch Hierarchy is based on the likelihood of each product switching to the other product groups and is ordered by “like” product. In cases where customers are moving balances between multiple deposit products, we apply a series of business rules to act as a tie breaker in determining which products are tagged as “switch.”

  1. First, we allocate switch between products that retain the same account number.
  2. Then, we use the switch hierarchy to the left to determine which product groups are most likely to switch into other product groups.
  3. If a customer is moving money between products within a product group, the switch hierarchy is determined by rate. We assume that the lowest rate products are most likely to Switch Out, and the highest rate products are most likely to see Switch In.

Click here to view the Switch Hierarchy for your institution.

We use a customizable methodology to handle cases when money flows from a closed term product to an existing liquid account.
  • This methodology captures switch only if a term account closes and an existing liquid account augments balance.
  • If a given customer has a CD disappear (at maturity or not), and an existing liquid product goes up by 15% or more of the closed CD’s value, we will tag that flow as switch into the liquid account.
  • This 15% threshold is customizable, but is conservative enough to eliminate noise in the data while still capturing the majority of customer behavior
Migrations from promotional / special to standard status in products will be captured as Change to Existing.
  • Liquid Products changing from promo product to standard product after the initial teaser period expires will be captured as Change to Existing (non-customer-initiated event).
    • Promo status identified by interest code
  • Term products rolling from special to standard products after the initial teaser period will be captured as Change to Existing (non-customer-initiated event).
    • Will roll into the lowest term of the product’s current term bucket (e.g. a 12 Month CD Special will roll into a 10-15 Month Standard CD).
    • Promo status identified by flag in data.
However, when a customer voluntarily re-ups an account in a promotional / special product, this flow will be captured as Switch.
  • Liquid product accounts switching from one promo product to another with the same product name will be captured as Switch (customer-initiated event).
    • Since interest code does not change, re-promo status will be identified by change in interest code effective date.
  • Term product accounts switching from one special product to another with the same product name will be captured as Switch (customer-initiated event).
    • Since the CD Special Indicator flag does not change, re-promo status will be identified by change in maturity date
 
 
Grace Period Adjustment Methodology

 

Our PriceTek Grace Period Adjustment methodology addresses data challenges caused by CD renewal grace periods.

CD Grace Period Challenges

  • Customers can have a small grace period, generally up to 10 days, following the maturity of an existing CD, in which they can revise a roll.
  • This is often used to reverse an automatic renewal event, by switching balances out of the account or rolling to a different product/term.
  • For CDs maturing towards the end of the month, there is the potential for EOM balances to reflect pre-finalized decisions. For example:
    • A customer’s CD matures and auto-renews on March 28th.
    • The decision is changed to withdraw all funds within the grace period on April 2nd.
    • The EOM balances seen in the March 31st data file will misleadingly suggest the account has “rolled”, causing March’s “roll rate” to be artificially high.
  • Note for CDs maturing early in a month, this grace period has no impact on the accuracy of month end statement balances. For example:
    • A customer’s CD matures and auto-renews on March 10th.
    • The decision is changed to withdraw all funds within the grace period on March 15th.
    • The EOM balances seen in the March 31st data file will accurately reflect the final decision.
We calculate term product roll rates using a three month time span, finalizing roll rates only when the subsequent month’s data is available.
