Vintage Data Methodology

Reference the sections below for the methodology behind PriceTek's Vintage Data Analytics. The Vintages process tags accounts based on their acquisition month (“Vintage”) and follows each through their full history, identifying balance runoff durations and providing an account and customer level historic dataset.

Introduction to Vintage Data Modules

Vintage Data Analytics tags accounts based on their acquisition year-month (“Vintage”) and follows each through their full history. The resulting dataset can be used to build balance runoff curves or other views tracking performance over time.

Vintage Data Analytics Module Module Capabilities Module Benefits
Summary
  • Flexibly view decay curves for any portfolio segment to determine what percent of balances or accounts remain x months after account opening.
  • Track retention performance of acquired portfolios or specific balance “Vintages.”
  • View portfolio balance analytics with “Vintage” as an identifier.
  • Identify the portfolio segments with the most favorable retention behavior to support marketing or product decisions.
  • Measure the success of balances associated with specific campaigns or promotions.

Customer Drill-down
  • Isolate specific groups of customers based on account and vintage attributes, and perform full portfolio analysis on those people over time

  • For example: “What does the full deposit wallet look like for all customers that participated in the January 2016 campaign, both before and after the campaign?”
  • Monitor whether or not promotional campaigns are effectively bringing in customers that grow their relationship with the bank.
  • Track the effectiveness of product fencing.
  • Develop a better understanding of the holistic customer relationship.
  • Generates a customer level dataset that can be leveraged for new custom reports.

 

Vintage Analytics: Key Concepts

Month Origination per Account

 

Vintage assignment is based on the month in which the account originated, and account attributes are tracked across time for every account

Understanding Decay
The decay of each “Vintage” can be seen across actual months, or aggregated and normalized to “months out” from origination.
 
By comparing balances in each Vintage back to their starting population, we’re able to identify the “% remaining” of each bucket for all months past origination. These can then be normalized to be viewed as “Months Out” from origination for easy comparison and analysis.

Understanding Account Origin

One key segmentation in Vintage Analytics is the Account Origin, or the manner in which an account originated in the data.
Vintage Analytics: Account Origin
Account Origin Description
Standard
  • Assigned to all accounts with known vintages, meaning the account appears for the first time within the historical dataset.
  • Further segmentation is available based on the PriceTek balance flow identified when the account first appears in the data:
    • Customer Acquisition
    • Account Acquisition
    • Switch In
  • Note: for the flow segmentation, only those accounts with a new account number will be included in the Vintage (e.g., accounts that Switch In but maintain an old account number will not be included in the Vintage of the month they switch).
Legacy
  • This origin is assigned to all accounts that are present in the very first month of data available to PriceTek, and are marked with this first month as their Vintage.
  • Further segmentation is not available as the originating flow can not be identified.
Bank Acquisitions
  • Any bank acquisitions which result in accounts appearing suddenly in the dataset that may not be newly originated in that month are assigned custom Account Origins.
  • Typically, these origins will only map to a single Vintage in the month they appeared.

 

PriceTek Vintage Key Definitions
 

Vintage Data Analytics references some new terminology:

  • Vintage: The date an account first appears in the data history, assigned monthly.
  • Account Origin: The manner in which an account has originated. Each account gets this label only once, when it first appears.
  • Vintage Exclusions: Specific accounts may be excluded from the analysis due to non-customer activity, outlier behavior or other data anomalies. Vintage exclusions are unique to the Vintages module, and can be hidden with the visible checkboxes.
  • Vintage Range: Represented in PriceTek with Earliest / Latest Vintage. This isolates the Vintages to be included in data output. Typically, Legacy and Bank Acquisition origins will only map to a single Vintage.
  • Date Range: Represented in PriceTek with Start Date / End Date. This isolates the months of data is included in the analysis. This differs from the Vintage Range. For example, one could analyze “accounts that originated in January 2013” (January ‘13 Vintage Range) across the full year 2013 (Date Range).
  • Months Out: Represents the number of months since account origination.
 

 

Vintage Core Concepts

There are several important concepts to keep in mind when viewing the Vintages datasets:

Vintage Core Concepts
Concept Details
All vintages are tracked based on starting account attributes

  • In most cases, at some point in an account’s life, the attributes (product name, product group, etc.) will change. Vintage threads are created and viewed based on starting attributes.

  • Listboxes in the Vintages modules filter for starting attributes.
Threads are tracked over time by account number

  • As long as an account number remains constant, the balances associated are “remaining” regardless of the product associated.

  • The thread stops when the account disappears, no switch flows are assumed.
Vintages are assigned when accounts appear for the first time in the dataset

  • Currently, Open Date is not used to assign vintages as the date the account appears has proven more reliable.

  • Accounts that exist in the first month of data, with an unknown age, are grouped separately using the Account Origin field.
The first month of new data from bank acquisitions is handled separately
  • Because the age of accounts in newly acquired datasets is unknown (i.e., from a bank acquisition), these threads are also tracked separately via the Account Origin field.
  • Balances that come organically into acquired branches post-acquisition are not isolated, as their origination date is apparent in the data.

 

Understanding the Vintage Data Summary Module
 

In PriceTek, the Summary module displays the duration that various balances or accounts remain with the bank, by product or other portfolio segments:

Module Answers:

  • What sort of balance duration can be expected across various product groups?
  • Do balances acquired into promotional or “teaser” products remain on book long enough to justify the campaign?
  • Do certain customer segments exhibit more balance stability?
  • Have we successfully retained balances from a recent bank or branch acquisition?
  • Does runoff vary for balances acquired in different years or months?

Vintage Thread Metric Aggregation

The Summary module uses two methods for averaging Percent Remaining data in the Metric Aggregation Drop-down:

  • When Months Out is selected as the X-Axis variable, Percent Balances Remaining and Percent Accounts Remaining can be averaged using Average of Vintages or Fully Weighted averaging technique.
    • Average of Vintages: The straight average percent remaining of each Vintage in the dataset. Each Vintage is calculated independently, and those values are then averaged.
    • Fully Weighted: Percent remaining is calculated by dividing the total remaining metric by the total metric’s starting value, independent of Vintage.
 

 

Understanding the Vintage Data Customer Drill-down Module
 

The Customer Drill-down module allows users to isolate specific groups of customers, and perform full portfolio analysis on those people over time:

Module Answers:

  • Have customers acquired during a specific campaign deepened their bank relationship since? Or increased overall balances?
  • What is the overall weighted rate paid on customers that were acquired in certain periods / products? How has it changed?
  • Did most customers that participated in a promotional campaign hold checking accounts prior? And afterwards?
  • How have customers inherited in bank or branch acquisitions adjusted their product mix since?
